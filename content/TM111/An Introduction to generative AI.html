1.2.1 - An Introduction to generative AI

Examples of AI in everyday life

  • Lots of us have been interacting with AI in various ways for years (sometimes without realising it).
  • Examples of AI in everyday life
    • Digital assistants (e.g., Siri, Alexa)
    • Recommendation systems (e.g., Netflix, Amazon)
    • Chatbots: used for customer service (shopping, banking, booking a holiday, tech support)
    • Facial recognition technology (e.g., unlocking phones, security systems)
    • Music and Media streaming services: (e.g., Spotify, Apple Music) Use AI to recommend songs and create playlists.
    • Online shopping: personalized product recommendations
    • Travel and navigation: (e.g., Google Maps, Waze) Use AI to provide real-time traffic updates and suggest the best routes.
    • Writing assistance: (e.g., Grammarly, Google Docs) Use AI to help with grammar, style, and content suggestions.
    • Health and Fitness apps: (e.g., MyFitnessPal, Fitbit) Use AI to provide personalized workout and nutrition recommendations.
    • Social media feeds: Use AI to curate content and suggest posts based on user preferences.

What is generative AI?

  • Generative AI refers to a type of artificial intelligence that can create new content, such as text, images, music, or even videos, based on the data it has been trained on.
  • Unlike traditional AI systems that are designed to perform specific tasks, generative AI models can generate original content that mimics human creativity.
  • Generative AI uses techniques such as deep learning and neural networks to analyze large datasets and learn patterns, structures, and relationships within the data.
  • Once trained, these models can produce new content by sampling from the learned patterns and generating outputs that are similar to the training data but not identical.
  • Examples of generative AI include language models like GPT-3 (which powers ChatGPT), image generation models like DALL-E, and music generation models like OpenAI's MuseNet.
  • It is a type of AI technology that automatically generates (or creates) content in response to prompts given to it by users

How does it work

  • Generative AI works by training on large datasets to learn patterns and structures within the data.
  • LLMs have been trained on vast amounts of data from webpages, social media conversations, and other online content to generate human-like responses to our prompts. These tools can continuously update with new data by collecting our previous questions and prompts.
  • Generative AI tools that deal with text are called Large language models (LLMs).
  • LLMs are designed to understand and generate human-like text based on the input they receive.
  • LLMs such as ChatGPT work like predictive text, except the algorithms are more sophisticated.
  • They create content based on recognized patterns. e.g what word or phrase is most likely to come next, rather than knowing what the words or phrases mean.
  • You can provide these tools with a prompt about almost any topic and they will respond in fluent English as well as many other languages, often sounding surprisingly knowledgeable, or with an image/computing code snippet.
  • Although outputs produced by Generative AI tool look plausible, they are often misleading, made up or maybe entirely wrong.
  • Each response is unique, you will never get the same reply twice
  • Responses are conversationbal, once you have asked a question you can just carry on the conversation by adding new questions or prompts, rather than having to redo your search, as you would in a search engine like google
