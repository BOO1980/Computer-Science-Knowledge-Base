3.1 – Wireless Networks

3.1.1 Video — Types of Wireless Networks

  • Our cellphones communicate on many different networks.
  • When we're communicating over the voice channel and we're talking, say to someone another cell phone, our communications are going through the cellular provider network.
  • When we use a Bluetooth device like a speaker or a headset or even a smartwatch, that device is communicating through a completely different network. It is a much shorter range wireless network than the cellular network and it works with different rules and different protocols.
  • Wi Fi is something you are probably the most familiar because Wi-fi is supported on many different types of devices, including smartphones.
  • When we are working on a Wi Fi network, we are usually using that network to communicate over the global Internet.
  • We also have near field communications allowing us to send information over an extremely short distance.
  • This is how we might use the smartphone to pay for something in a grocery store.
  • All these communications protocols, rules and frequencies are contained within the same device.
  • Your cellphone is also capable of receiving GPS signals. Meaning that your cellphone is actually receiving signals from satellites at our stations all around the earth.
  • In summary, our mobile devices are equipped to communicate over a variety of wireless networks, each with its own unique characteristics and protocols.

3.1.3 Other Wireless Networks

  • In addition to the GSM and 4G5G transmitters and receivers, smartphones make connection in a variety of ways.
  • Global Positioning System: The GPS uses satellites to transmit signals that cover the globe. The smartphone can receive these signals and calculate the phone's location to an accuracy of within 10 meters.
  • Wi fi:
    • Wi Fi transmitters and receivers located within the smartphone enables a phone to connect to local networks and Internet.
    • In order to receive and send data on the Wi Fi network, the phone needs to be within the range of the signal from a wireless network access point.
    • Wi Fi networks are usually privately owned, but often provide guest or public access hotpots.
    • A hotspot is an area where Wi Fi signals are available.
    • Wi Fi networks connection on the phone and similar to the network connections on a laptop computer.
  • Bluetooth:
    • Bluetooth is a low power, short range wireless technology that is intended to replace wired connectivity for accessories such as speakers, headphones and microphones.
    • Bluetooth can also be used to connect a smart watch to a smartphone.
    • Bluetooth technology can be used to transmit both data and voice. It can be used to create small local networks.
    • Bluetooth is wireless technology allows devices to communicate over short distances.
    • Multiple devices can be connected at the same time with Bluetooth.
  • Near Field Communication (NFC):
    • NFC is a wireless communication technology that enables data to be exchanged by devices that are in a very close proximity to each other. Usually less than a few CM.
    • For example, NFC can be used to connect a smartphone and a payment system.
    • NFC uses electromagnetic fields to transmit data.

2.1.3 Peer-to-Peer Networks

2.1.4 Peer-to-peer applications.

  • A P2P application allows a device to act as both a client and a server within the same communication.
  • In this model, every client is the server and every server is a client.
  • P2P applications require that each end device provider user interface and run a background service.
  • Applications use a hybrid system where resource sharing is decentralized, but the indexes that point to resource locations are stored in a centralized directory.
  • In a hybrid system, each peer accesses an index server to get the location of a resource stored on another peer.
