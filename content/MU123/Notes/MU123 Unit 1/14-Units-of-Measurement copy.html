Units of Measurement

  • Many everyday calculations involve measurements of some kind.
  • For example, lengths, times, amounts of money and so on.
  • In the UK both the metric and imperial systems of measurement are used.
  • In the metric system, the different units for the same type of quantity are related to each other via powers of ten: for example, 1 meter is the same as 100 (or 102) centimeters.
  • In the US, metre is spelled METER.
  • In the Imperial system, the units are related in different ways. For example, one stone is the same as 14 pounds.
  • The metric system was founded in France in the wake of the French Revolution, and work on SI units has been ongoing since the middle of the 20th century.
  • To the time of writing that only countries who have not adopted SI units as their sole or primary system of measurement of the United States, Burma and Liberia.
  • There are seven base senior units from which all the other units are derived.
  • The base units are:
    • metre (m)
    • kilogram (kg)
    • second (s)
    • ampere (A)
    • kelvin (K)
    • mole (mol)
    • candela (cd)
  • When you make a measurement, it is sometimes helpful to round your answer. Rounding can make it easier to work with the number and can help to avoid errors in calculations.

Converting units

  • To convert between different units of the same type of quantity, you need to know the relationship between the two units.
  • For example, to convert from meters to centimeters, you need to know that 1 meter is the same as 100 centimeters.
  • To convert from one unit to another, you can use a conversion factor. A conversion factor is a fraction that represents the relationship between two units.
  • For example, to convert from meters to centimeters, you can use the conversion factor 100 cm / 1 m. To convert a measurement in meters to centimeters, you multiply the measurement by the conversion factor:
  • Measurement in cm = Measurement in m × (100 cm / 1 m)
  • For example, to convert 2.5 meters to centimeters:
  • Measurement in cm = 2.5 m × (100 cm / 1 m) = 250 cm
  • You can also use conversion factors to convert from larger units to smaller units. For example, to convert from kilometers to meters, you can use the conversion factor 1000 m / 1 km. To convert a measurement in kilometers to meters, you multiply the measurement by the conversion factor:
  • Measurement in m = Measurement in km × (1000 m / 1 km)
  • For example, to convert 3.2 kilometers to meters:
  • Measurement in m = 3.2 km × (1000 m / 1 km) = 3200 m
  • To convert from one unit to another, you should first find out how many of the smaller units are equivalent to one of the larger units.
  • If the units are metric, then you can tell this from the prefixes. For example, 1 kilometer is equal to 1000 meters, so the prefix "kilo-" indicates a factor of 1000.
  • If you want to convert to the smaller unit, then there will be more of these units, so you need to multiply by this number.
  • If you want to convert to the larger unit then there will be few of these units. So you need to divide by the number.
  • A few other metric units are commonly used alongside the SI units. These include:
    • Electric tonne (t) - a unit of mass equal to 1000 kilograms.
    • Liter (L) - a unit of volume equal to 1 cubic decimeter.
    • Kelvin (K) - a unit of temperature.
  • Time is also often measured in non metric units. You have probably never heard of a kilo second.
  • The UK has embraced metric units rather less enthusiastically than most other countries, and many of its traditional imperial units are still used.
  • If you live in the UK, you may be comfortable with measuring kitchen cabinets in millimeters and buying petrol in liters.
  • But you may think of your height in feet and inches and your weight in stones and pounds.
  • More seriously. NASA lost the Mars Climate Orbiter spacecraft in 1999 as a result of an error caused because one team working in the project used Imperial new units of management, while others use metric.
  • In 1969, the UK government set up the Metrication Board with the aim of ensuring substantial adoption of metric units in the UK by 1975.
