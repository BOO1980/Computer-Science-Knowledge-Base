Fractions

  • A fraction is a way to represent a part of a whole.
  • The top number (numerator) represents how many parts we have.
  • A fraction is written as one number over another and means the top number divided by the bottom number.
  • Fractions which are equal in value but are written with different numerators and denominators are called equivalent fractions.
  • You can find equivalent fractions for any given fraction by multiplying top and bottom by the same whole number.
  • Decimal themselves are a form of fraction. They are situations. Where it is simpler to use fractions.
  • The bottom number (denominator) represents how many equal parts the whole is divided into.
  • Fractions can be proper (numerator < denominator), improper (numerator> denominator), or mixed (a whole number and a proper fraction).
  • Fractions can be added, subtracted, multiplied, and divided using specific rules.
  • A fraction. Is in its simplest form if the numerator and the denominator have no common factors other than 1.
  • Addition and subtracting with fractions can be quite awkward, particularly when fractions are mixed with whole numbers and when the fractions have different denominators.
  • To add two fractions with the same denominator, just add the numerators.
  • To subtract one fraction from another where the dominators are the same, just subtract 1 numerator from the other.
  • If the dominators are different, the calculation is a bit more awkward. The first thing to do is to rewrite the factions as equivalent factions where the dominators are the same.
  • Sometimes you have to find equivalent fractions for all fractions in some in order that the denominators are the same.
  • To multiply factions, multiply the denominators together and the numerators together, canceling any factors which the numerators and denominators have in common.
  • Before considering division of fractions, it is helpful to think about division of whole numbers.
  • To divide by a fraction, turn the fraction upside down and multiply.
  • Turning a fraction upside down is called finding each reciprocal.
  • Division by number is the same as multiplication by its reciprocal.
  • In order to arithmetic with mixed numbers, it is often best to write them as a simple fraction that is one number over another.
  • Multiplication or division involving mixed numbers is done by converting them to fractions first.
  • Fractions are also important in mathematics, particularly in algebra.
  • A fraction is a number that describes the relationship between part of something and the whole.
  • The top number in a fraction is called the numerator and the bottom number is called the denominator
  • To find a fraction equivalent to a given fraction. Multiply or divide the numerator and denominator by the same non zero number.

    • Simplifying fractions.

    • When you divide the top and bottom of a fraction by a whole number larger than one, you get an equivalent fraction with a small numerator and denominator. This is known as canceling the fraction.
    • When a fraction has been canceled to give the smallest possible numerator and denominator but still whole numbers is said to be in its simplest form or lowest terms.

  • They can make statistics more relatable and easier to understand.

Mixed numbers and improper fractions

  • A mixed number is a whole number combined with a proper fraction. For example, 2 1/2 is a mixed number.
  • An improper fraction is a fraction where the numerator is greater than or equal to the denominator. For example, 5/4 is an improper fraction.

Fractions of Quantities

  • To find a fraction of a quantity, multiply the quantity by the numerator and divide by the denominator. For example, to find 3/4 of 20, calculate (3 * 20) / 4 = 15.
  • Sometimes you need to calculate fractions of quantities. For example, if you have a recipe that serves eight people, and you want to make it for three people or thirty people, say, then you have to scale the quantities of the ingredients.
  • In theory you can scale any recipe to cater for a group of any size, but in practice you may wish to adjust your answers a little.
  • In general, when you are using mathematics to make practical decisions, it is important to think about whether your calculations are appropriate for the situation
